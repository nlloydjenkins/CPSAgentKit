# Accessibility Standards – Outbound Email Correspondence

## Purpose

This document defines the formatting and language standards that the Accessibility Presenter agent applies to all outbound emails. These standards are derived from the British Dyslexia Association (BDA) Style Guide 2023, plain English principles, and general accessibility best practice. The goal is to ensure every email the organisation sends is readable and understandable by the widest possible audience, including people with dyslexia, low literacy, or cognitive fatigue.

The Accessibility Presenter does not alter the meaning, intent, or compliance-approved content of an email. It restructures and reformats only.

---

## Reading Level

Target reading age: 9–11 years (Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level ≤ 5).

This does not mean the content should be patronising. It means sentences should be short, words should be common, and structure should be predictable. Complex ideas can be communicated simply — the constraint is on language complexity, not on the sophistication of what is being communicated.

---

## Sentence and Paragraph Rules

- Maximum sentence length: 15–20 words. If a sentence exceeds 20 words, split it.
- Maximum paragraph length: 3–4 sentences. A single-sentence paragraph is acceptable and often preferable for key information.
- One idea per sentence. Do not chain clauses with semicolons or colons.
- Use active voice. Replace "Your application has been received by our team" with "We have received your application."
- Avoid double negatives. Replace "We cannot process your request without the required documents" with "We need the required documents to process your request."
- Avoid conditional chains. Replace "If you have not already provided your account number, and if your application relates to an existing account, then you will need to supply this before we can proceed" with "We need your account number to continue. Please send it in your reply."

## Abbreviations and Jargon

- Avoid abbreviations where possible.
- If an abbreviation must be used, write the full term first followed by the abbreviation in brackets: "Financial Conduct Authority (FCA)." After the first use, the abbreviation may be used alone.
- Do not assume the reader knows internal terminology. Terms like "workflow," "escalation," "triage queue," or "SLA" should be replaced with plain language or explained on first use.
- Product names, form references, or regulatory citations should be kept if they are necessary for the reader to take action, but explained in context: "Please complete form AP-3 (the account change request form)."

## Email Structure

Every outbound email should follow a predictable structure so the reader can scan for the information they need.

### Structure Template

1. **What this is about** — one sentence identifying the purpose of the email and the applicant's reference number.
2. **What you need to do** — the specific action(s) required, if any. Each action on a separate line or bullet. Deadlines stated clearly.
3. **What we have received** — a brief summary of what the applicant has already provided, so they know we have it and do not repeat it.
4. **What happens next** — what the organisation will do and when the applicant can expect to hear back.
5. **How to get help** — contact details or instructions for reaching a person if the applicant is unsure about anything.

If no action is required from the applicant (e.g., an acknowledgement email), sections 2 and 3 may be omitted.

### Structural Rules

- Place the most important information first. If the reader stops reading after the first two sentences, they should still know what to do.
- Use short headings or bold lead-ins to separate sections. Do not rely on paragraph breaks alone.
- Left-align all text. Do not use justified alignment — uneven spacing between words makes text harder to read for people with dyslexia.
- Avoid walls of text. If a section exceeds 4–5 lines, break it up.

## Formatting for Dyslexia

These rules are derived from the BDA Style Guide 2023 and apply to the text content of the email. Some formatting constraints (font, colour, background) are controlled by the email template rather than the agent, but the agent should apply all content-level formatting rules.

### Content-Level Rules (Applied by the Agent)

- Short paragraphs: 3–4 sentences maximum.
- Line spacing: leave a blank line between paragraphs. Do not use single-spaced continuous prose.
- Avoid blocks of capitalised text. Use sentence case for headings. ALL CAPS reads as shouting and is harder to scan for dyslexic readers.
- Avoid italics for emphasis. Use bold sparingly for key terms or deadlines.
- Avoid underlining except for hyperlinks, to prevent confusion.
- Numbered lists or bullet points for multiple items or steps. Keep list items short (one line each if possible).
- Avoid starting a sentence at the end of a line where it wraps to the next — this is controlled by line length but the agent should avoid overly long sentences that make this more likely.

### Template-Level Rules (Applied by Email Template, Not the Agent)

These are included for completeness and should be configured in the organisation's email template:

- Font: sans-serif (Arial, Calibri, Verdana, or Tahoma). Size 12–14pt.
- Background: cream or soft pastel, not pure white. White can appear too bright and cause visual stress.
- Text colour: dark grey (#333333) or dark blue (#1a1a2e) on light background. Avoid pure black on pure white.
- Do not use green or red/pink text — these are difficult for people with colour vision deficiencies.

---

## Tone

- Warm but professional. The reader should feel that a person wrote this, not a system.
- Direct but not abrupt. "We need your account number" is fine. "Account number missing. Provide immediately" is not.
- Reassuring where appropriate. If an application is progressing normally, say so: "Everything is on track."
- Never blame the reader for incomplete information. "We still need a few details" not "You failed to provide the following."
- Avoid urgency language unless a genuine deadline exists. "As soon as possible" is vague and creates anxiety. "By 15 April" is clear.

## Worked Example

### Before (Drafter Output)

> Dear Mr Thompson,
>
> I am writing to acknowledge receipt of your application dated 12 March 2026, reference APP-2026-0847. Upon review of the documentation furnished, we have ascertained that certain information required in order to process your application has not been provided. Specifically, we require your account number pertaining to the existing account in respect of which the amendment is sought, together with the effective date from which you wish the amendment to commence. In the event that we do not receive the aforementioned information within 10 business days, we will be unable to proceed with your application and it may consequently be closed. Should you have any queries regarding the above, please do not hesitate to contact us.

### After (Accessibility Presenter Output)

> **Your application APP-2026-0847**
>
> Thank you for your application on 12 March 2026.
>
> **What we need from you**
>
> We need two more details to continue:
>
> 1. Your account number (the account you want to change).
> 2. The date you want the change to start.
>
> Please reply to this email with these details by **26 March 2026**.
>
> **What we have received**
>
> We have your name and contact details. You do not need to send these again.
>
> **What happens next**
>
> Once we have the missing details, we will process your application. We aim to complete this within 5 working days.
>
> If you have any questions, reply to this email or call us on 0800 123 4567.
