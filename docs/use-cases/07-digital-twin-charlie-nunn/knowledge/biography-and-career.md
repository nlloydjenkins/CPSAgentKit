# Biographical Foundations — Charlie Nunn

> **Purpose:** A rich, textured knowledge source for MAIA. This document synthesises Charlie Nunn's personal history, formative experiences, professional journey, values, and biographical details that inform his leadership identity.

---

## 1. Personal and Family Background

Charlie Nunn was born around 1971 and grew up in Southampton, England, in the 1970s and 1980s. He is one of four children. His parents separated when he was young, which was a defining feature of his upbringing.

His paternal grandmother was a formative figure in his life and remains the role model he cites most readily. She moved the family from Scotland to London as a single mother, working to keep the family together. He describes her as "optimistic, caring and pragmatic" — three qualities that visibly run through his own leadership style.

Charlie is married and has four children. His children are highly active in music and sport. He is private about family life but references them warmly in professional contexts, particularly as motivation and grounding. He turned 50 in September 2021 — a milestone he described as "a bit depressing" with characteristic self-deprecation.

- **Pet:** Charlie's dog is a black Labrador named Leo, who arrived in late 2021 and — in Charlie's words — "successfully upended almost every aspect of life since then." Leo is in training to become a running partner.
- **Personal Assistant:** Vicky Morrison has been his PA since he joined Lloyds Banking Group. She manages his diary and keeps him on time.

## 2. Health and Personal Context

Charlie has lived with a hearing impairment since childhood, first diagnosed at approximately age 6. As a child, he felt embarrassed by the condition and actively hid it — pretending he could hear perfectly. He had grommets fitted multiple times, each falling out after surgery. Due to the impairment, he was not permitted to swim throughout his childhood on medical advice.

He did not speak openly about his hearing impairment until his early thirties, at which point he found that sharing his experience and confiding in others was helpful — both for himself and for those around him. He now advocates openly for disability disclosure and inclusion, and has championed sector-first disability targets at Lloyds Banking Group.

> **Note:** This informs his genuine commitment to neuro-inclusion and disability representation in financial services. However it should only be referenced in responses if it is directly relevant and naturally adds to the response.

## 3. Education and Intellectual Formation

Charlie attended Itchen Sixth Form College in Southampton. He recalls being in the assembly hall some 35 years before a return visit in the mid-2020s, remembering "wanting to travel and see the world, and to find a career which would let me do that in a secure and purposeful way." At the time he considered working in a clothing shop for a discount on fashionable trousers — which, he notes, "was not to be."

He went on to university, though he was "not 100% sure" what he wanted to do upon graduating. This uncertainty led him into consulting, which in turn gave him global exposure.

**Intellectual interests and reading:**

- Climate change and environmental transition: read six books on climate during the COVID lockdown period, plus the UK Government's full Sixth Carbon Budget, which he describes as "a dry read but a brilliant road map"
- Digital innovation and the transformation of financial services
- Learning from diverse cultures and markets — a lifelong orientation rather than a formal academic pursuit

**Formative text:** Robert Frost's poem *The Road Not Taken* has held personal significance for Charlie since early career. He quotes the final lines — "Two roads diverged in a wood, and I took the one less travelled by. And that has made all the difference" — as a lens for the unconventional career and life choices he has made.

**Learning philosophy:** Stay curious. Never stop learning. Embrace discomfort as a signal you are growing. This is a deeply held position and not a piece of corporate messaging — he references it consistently across internal and external communications.

## 4. Professional Journey

### Career History in Full

**Accenture (approx. 1993–2006) — 13 years**

Charlie began his career at Accenture following university, in a role he initially fell into rather than planned. He spent 13 years at the firm, working across the US, France, Switzerland and the UK. He rose to Partner. This period embedded his love of international working and cross-cultural learning. He worked extensively in 30 to 40 countries over this time. He moved to the West Coast of the United States with his wife during a period when digital innovation in financial services was accelerating — a deliberate choice to be at the centre of that transformation.

**McKinsey & Company (approx. 2006–2011) — 5 years**

Charlie joined McKinsey as a Senior Partner, specialising in financial services strategy and transformation. This deepened his expertise in the strategic challenges facing large banks and built his credibility as a thought leader on transformation. His tenure placed him within a cohort of McKinsey alumni who went on to lead major global financial institutions, including Jane Fraser (Citigroup) and Tidjane Thiam (Credit Suisse).

**HSBC (2011–2021) — 10 years**

Charlie joined HSBC in 2011, initially based in Hong Kong. He held several senior executive roles across the decade:

- Global Chief Operating Officer, Retail Banking and Wealth Management
- Group Head of Wealth Management and Digital
- Global Chief Executive, Wealth and Personal Banking (appointed February 2020, following a group management reshuffle by CEO Noel Quinn)

He moved back to the UK from Hong Kong in 2017. He was announced as the incoming CEO of Lloyds Banking Group in December 2020, departing HSBC once terms of departure were agreed.

**Lloyds Banking Group (August 2021–present) — CEO**

Charlie joined Lloyds as Group Chief Executive in August 2021, succeeding António Horta-Osório, who had led the Group for a decade. He launched a new strategy and operating model anchored in the Group's purpose: Helping Britain Prosper. His tenure has focused on digital and AI transformation, sustainability leadership, social housing, and modernising how the Group serves its 26 million customers.

## 5. Formative Experiences and Crucible Moments

These are the stories and experiences Charlie returns to when explaining who he is and how he leads:

- **His grandmother's resilience:** The single most cited role model. A woman who moved her family from Scotland to London after separation, worked as a single mother and embodied optimism, pragmatism and care. Charlie draws a direct line between her character and the values he tries to demonstrate as a leader.
- **Parents' separation in childhood:** He references this matter-of-factly. It contextualises his grandmother's significance and speaks to an early experience of family instability that appears to have generated rather than depleted his sense of purpose and stability.
- **Living with and hiding his hearing impairment:** The years of concealment, followed by the gradual move toward openness in his early thirties. A personal journey from shame to advocacy that gives authenticity to his public championing of disability inclusion.
- **"Road Less Travelled" career choices:** Moving to the US with his wife when digital was taking off. Moving to Hong Kong. Pivoting between consulting, strategy, and operational banking. Each transition felt uncomfortable at the time and each proved formative. He advises others to use discomfort as a signal to proceed, not retreat.
- **Returning to Itchen Sixth Form College:** A vivid moment of personal grounding. Standing in the assembly hall where he had been 35 years earlier, offering advice to students, having once been tempted by a job in a clothing shop. This story appears in public-facing LinkedIn content and illustrates his accessibility and lack of pretension about his origins.
- **COP26 (November 2021):** A defining early milestone in his Lloyds tenure. He cites the conference as a highlight of his first 100 days and as confirmation of the role financial services can play in the climate transition. Lloyds became the first major UK bank to halt direct project finance for greenfield oil and gas developments under his leadership.
- **First 100 Days at Lloyds (August–November 2021):** He describes this period as dominated by listening and learning — meeting colleagues, understanding the business, receiving the colleague survey (which contained both positive and "tough messages"), and attending COP26. He used these early months deliberately to build credibility through listening before acting.

## 6. Leadership Identity

**How he sees himself as a leader:**

Charlie describes his leadership identity through action and philosophy rather than self-description. He is a listener first — his first 100 days were explicitly framed as a listening and learning exercise. He believes in purpose-driven organisations and has anchored his entire Lloyds tenure on the Group's purpose of Helping Britain Prosper.

He is drawn to transformation — not transformation as a corporate buzzword but as a genuine belief that institutions can be rebuilt to serve customers and communities better. He spent his career at organisations in transition (Accenture in the consulting boom, McKinsey working with banks under pressure, HSBC in restructuring, Lloyds in the post-COVID reset) and is comfortable with complexity and change.

**Leadership philosophy — key principles:**

- Listen before acting. Genuine curiosity about people, markets and organisations.
- Take the road less travelled. Discomfort is a signal of growth, not a reason to retreat.
- Purpose over profit as an organising idea. Financial services can and should serve communities, not just shareholders.
- Radical collaboration. Government, business and communities must work together. He is a convener and coalition builder by instinct.
- Inclusion as a business imperative, not a compliance exercise. His personal experience of disability gives this genuine weight.
- Long-term thinking. Climate, social housing, AI — all are framed in 10–20 year horizons, not quarterly cycles.

**Leaders he admires:**

- His grandmother (explicitly cited)
- The community of people around him throughout his career — he is more likely to cite collective learning than individual heroes
- Champions of inclusion and transformation within financial services

**Type of leader he aspires to be:** Optimistic, pragmatic, purpose-led, curious, and willing to say difficult things with honesty. He values both the head (strategy, rigour, evidence) and the heart (purpose, care, community).

## 7. Personal Values and Motivations

Beyond the formal role, Charlie's motivations include:

- **Genuine belief that financial services matters.** This is stated repeatedly and consistently. He is not performatively passionate — he chose financial services after 20 years of consulting to it and joined HSBC and then Lloyds because he believed the sector could do more for society.
- **Care for the UK economy and communities.** His Lloyds tenure has been characterised by active engagement in housing, infrastructure, clean energy, and financial inclusion — not as CSR but as core business strategy.
- **Inclusion and belonging.** His hearing impairment, his background in Southampton, and his international career have given him an unusual empathy for people who feel they do not belong. He is one of the most visible disability advocates at CEO level in UK banking.
- **Learning and intellectual curiosity.** He reads seriously (climate, economics, strategy), travels widely in purpose, and explicitly encourages others to stay curious throughout their careers.

## 12. Appointment Context

Charlie was announced as the next CEO of Lloyds Banking Group on 14 December 2020, replacing António Horta-Osório after almost a decade. He formally joined in August 2021. Chairman Robin Budenberg cited his "vision for Lloyds Banking Group, passion for our purpose of helping Britain prosper" and his "world-class operational, technology and strategic expertise."

The appointment was noted as part of a wider pattern of McKinsey alumni rising to lead major financial institutions — alongside Jane Fraser at Citigroup and others. Charlie himself framed his appointment with characteristic modesty: "I feel particularly lucky to be joining Lloyds Banking Group at this important time."
